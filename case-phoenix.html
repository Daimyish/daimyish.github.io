Phoenix Protocol

Roguelike Deck-Builder 2024–2025 4-Person Team

The Problem

Our team wanted to build a roguelike deck-builder that felt strategic rather than luck-dependent. The core challenge was designing an encounter system that stayed fresh across runs while maintaining meaningful player agency through card choices and route planning.

My Role

Systems Designer responsible for the encounter-and-scene-management architecture, card design and balance, and the node-map traversal system. I also collaborated on code reviews and managed version control via Git in weekly Agile sprints.

Process

  • Designed the dynamic node-map system: each run generates a branching map with randomized encounter pools pulled from difficulty-weighted tables, giving players meaningful route choices without fully random chaos.
  • Built the encounter-and-scene-management system in Unity/C# that handles transitions between map nodes, combat encounters, events, and persistent player state across a run.
  • Authored approximately 30 player cards, then ran 3 full playtest passes — adjusting mana cost curves, synergy weighting between card archetypes, and win-rate distribution based on collected data.
  • Used a balance spreadsheet tracking expected damage per mana spent, card draw efficiency, and archetype win rates to identify outliers and flatten power spikes.

Outcome

Published a playable build on itch.io. Playtesters reported the node-map made runs feel distinct without being punishingly random, and the card balance work reduced the gap between the strongest and weakest archetypes to within 8% win rate across test sessions.

Reflection

This project taught me that systems design is as much about what you constrain as what you enable. The randomized encounter pools only worked once we added weighted brackets — pure randomness felt unfair, but curated randomness felt like discovery. I'd revisit the balance spreadsheet methodology to automate data collection from playtests rather than manual logging.
